    PITTSBURG -- The city's Old Town has a new look, and an iconic old standby is well on its way to a fresher look of its own.

    The New Mecca Cafe, founded in the mid-'60s, is using a $3.3 million loan from the city's redevelopment agency to open new dining space in an adjacent building, remodel its present building and install an outdoor seating area.

    Construction on the new dining room should be completed in February, according to assistant city manager Joe Sbranti.

    After that, the restaurant will operate in the new dining room until the renovation of the old dining room is completed next summer.

    "The Mecca is still operating while we are remodeling what used to be referred to as the Martinetti building," Sbranti said.

    A new kitchen in the Martinetti building will serve the entire restaurant when the project is completed and the old kitchen will be torn out, according to project manager Jim Miller.

    The restaurant's capacity will increase from 50 to about 100 when the entire project is finished, Miller said.

    Plans include adding tile and removing lava rock from the front of the building. The interior will have a tile floor and tin ceiling, Miller said.

    "The exterior will return to its historic look, with a lot of windows, tile above the windows and awnings, and exposed brick," he said.

    The city, which has undertaken several redevelopment projects downtown in recent years, is widening the sidewalk along

    Third Street north of the Mecca in part to accommodate the outdoor seating area, Sbranti said. Traffic in the area is being detoured during the work.

    Owner Guillermo Muniz traded property he owned on the block to the city for the Martinetti building.

    Pittsburg decided in 2007 to preserve the buildings in the block that is bounded by Third and Fourth streets, Railroad Avenue and Black Diamond Street in approving any development.

    The loan will be forgiven if the restaurant operates for 10 more years.

    The New Mecca has a Bay Area-wide reputation for its Mexican food, in part because of Muniz's catering for the Oakland A's and the San Francisco Giants and 49ers.

    Muniz said many pro athletes look forward to eating his cuisine when their teams visit the Bay Area.

    "In Oakland, we provide the clubhouse food for one game in every homestand," he said. "We've been doing it for about 20 years."

    Muniz, from Guadalajara, Mexico, started out at the New Mecca part-time, making tortillas and washing dishes while he also worked at the now-defunct Pittsburg cannery. He bought the restaurant in the late '70s.

    "The owner wasn't interested in operating it, and he offered it to me," Muniz said.

    He's made upgrades to the food and presentation over the years.

    "We cook with corn oil so it's more healthy," Muniz said. "We've improved everything, cooking every day in the morning, afternoon and evening (for freshness)."
